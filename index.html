Demo Playbook
Excel AI · Sales team reference
01
Qualify the pain (pre-call email or intake form)
▶
Goal: arrive at the demo already knowing which 2–3 use cases will land hardest
Question 1
What does your team spend the most time doing manually in spreadsheets when dealing with text data like survey responses?
Opens the door without leading. Let them name their pain. Don't guess it for them.
Question 2
How many rows of text data do you typically process in a batch?
Establishes scale. If it's 50 rows, the pain is mild. If it's 10K+, you're solving a real time problem.
Question 3
Have you tried using ChatGPT or another AI tool for this? What broke?
Surfaces their objections early: copy-paste fatigue, inconsistent outputs, data privacy fears. You address these in the demo.
Question 4
How sensitive is the data you are working with? Would it need to stay local?
If yes → the local LM Studio angle becomes the headline feature. If no → you can focus on speed and ease.
Question 5
Who would actually use this day-to-day? Analysts, ops people, managers? How technical are they?
Tells you whether to emphasize "no code required" or "configurable models." Shapes your demo language.
Ask them to send a sample. "Can you send us 10–20 rows of real (or anonymized) data you'd want to process?" Then you build the demo around their actual data.
02
Open the demo: mirror their pain back (2 min)
▶
Goal: make them feel heard, then set up the "what if" moment
Say this
"You told us your team spends lots of time manually [doing X]. Let me show you what that looks like with one formula…"
Restate their exact words from discovery. This isn't a feature tour. It's a mirror.
Then ask
"If this could handle [their task] on 1,000 rows while you grab coffee, what does your team do with that time back?"
Forces them to visualize the outcome before they see the product. Now they're emotionally invested.
Don't open Excel yet. Spend 2 minutes in conversation. The demo hasn't started until they've articulated what "good" looks like.
03
The live demo: 3 use cases, escalating complexity (15 min)
▶
Pick the 3 that match their pain. Click a use case below to see the demo script.
Sentiment analysis: demo script
Show first
Type =EXCEL.AI("Classify sentiment as Positive, Negative, or Neutral. Reply with only one word.", A5) in B5 next to a real review. Let the cell populate live.
Then drag down
Select B5, drag down 12 rows. Pause. Let the silence do the selling while cells fill in.
Ask during
"How long would this take your team to do manually for a batch of 5,000 survey responses?"
Twist the knife
"Now watch. I'll change the prompt to also extract the top complaint keyword." Edit the prompt, drag again. Same data, richer output, zero extra setup.
Best for
Market research firms, CX teams, survey platforms, product teams reading NPS verbatims.
Data extraction: demo script
Show first
Use a messy email-like text in A5. Type =EXCEL.AI("Extract the company name only", A5) in B5. Then in C5: =EXCEL.AI("Extract the dollar amount only", A5).
The aha moment
Add a 4th column: "Extract the date and convert to YYYY-MM-DD format." This shows the AI handles formatting too. It's not just extraction, it's transformation.
Ask during
"How much of your team's time goes into parsing emails, invoices, or notes into structured data?"
Best for
Finance teams, legal ops, procurement, anyone drowning in unstructured documents.
Categorization: demo script
Show first
Show the expense list. Type =EXCEL.AI("Categorize this expense into one of: Travel, Software, Office, Meals, Marketing. Reply with one word.", A5).
Escalate
Add a second column: "Is this tax deductible for a US-based LLC? Yes or No." Two columns, one drag, two answers per row.
Ask during
"What categories does your team use? Could you swap these labels for yours and run this on your own data today?"
Best for
Finance, accounting, ops teams who manually tag transactions, support tickets, or log entries.
Data cleaning: demo script
Show first
Show the messy inputs column. Type =EXCEL.AI("Clean and normalize this into a standard format", A5). Watch "john.doe AT gmail DOT com" become a proper email.
The hook
"This would normally take a regex wizard an hour to handle all the edge cases. The AI just reads the intent."
Ask during
"What's the messiest dataset your team deals with regularly? CRM imports? Vendor lists?"
Best for
Data teams, anyone doing CRM migrations, analytics prep, or merging datasets from multiple sources.
Summarization: demo script
Show first
Paste long meeting notes in A5. In B5: =EXCEL.AI("Summarize in one sentence", A5). In C5: =EXCEL.AI("Extract action items as a comma-separated list", A5).
Escalate
"Now imagine this column has 200 meeting transcripts from last quarter. Your team gets a dashboard of every decision and action item in minutes."
Ask during
"How does your team currently track decisions and follow-ups from meetings?"
Best for
Consulting firms, project management, executive teams, anyone with call transcripts piling up.
Translation: demo script
Show first
Product description in A5. B5: =EXCEL.AI("Translate to Spanish", A5). C5: French. D5: German. Drag all three columns down simultaneously.
The visual punch
Seeing 8 products × 3 languages fill in at once is viscerally impressive. Let it run in silence.
Ask during
"What's your current localization workflow? How much do you spend per language on translation services?"
Best for
E-commerce, SaaS with international users, marketing teams managing multi-language content.
Email drafts: demo script
Show first
Show name + company + notes in columns A–C. In D5: =EXCEL.AI("Write a short, warm sales follow-up email using the contact name, company, and context.", A5:C5).
The hook
Each email is different because each row's context is different. Point this out: "It's not a mail merge. Each one is genuinely personalized."
Ask during
"How many outbound emails does your team send per week? How much time goes into personalizing each one?"
Best for
Sales teams, SDRs, account managers, recruiting teams doing outbound.
Resume screening: demo script
Show first
Candidate summary + role in A–B. In C5: =EXCEL.AI("Score this candidate 1-10 for the role. Reply with only a number.", A5:B5). Then D5 for strengths, E5 for concerns.
Key nuance
Emphasize this is a first-pass filter, not a decision maker. The AI handles volume so humans can focus on the top 20%.
Ask during
"How many applications do you review per open role? What's the bottleneck? Volume or consistency?"
Best for
HR teams, recruiting agencies, high-volume hiring orgs.
Product copy: demo script
Show first
Product name + specs in A–B. In C5: =EXCEL.AI("Write a 2-sentence marketing description", A5:B5). In D5: =EXCEL.AI("Generate 5 SEO keywords, comma-separated", A5:B5).
Escalate
"Now imagine you have 500 SKUs launching next quarter. Each one needs copy and keywords. This is done before lunch."
Ask during
"How many new products or SKUs do you launch per month? Who writes the copy today?"
Best for
E-commerce, retail, CPG brands, marketplace sellers managing large catalogs.
Formula helper: demo script
When to show
Use this as a crowd-pleaser at the end, not a lead use case. It shows breadth but doesn't show the core value prop (bulk text processing).
Show
Type =EXCEL.AI("Write an Excel formula: look up the price from column B where column A matches D1"). The AI returns a working VLOOKUP or XLOOKUP.
Positioning
"This is the Swiss Army knife angle. Even when you're not processing text data, the AI function is useful for everyday Excel tasks."
The 3-use-case arc: Start with their #1 pain (usually sentiment or extraction). Then show a related use case they didn't think of. Then finish with something unexpected that widens the aperture. The progression should feel like: "Oh it solves my problem" → "Oh it solves problems I didn't even think to ask about" → "Wait, what else can this do?"
04
The "hand them the keyboard" moment (5 min)
▶
Goal: get them to use the product during the call. This is where pull happens
Say this
"What's something your team does every week that you wish was automated? Type the prompt. Anything. Let's see if it works right now."
The moment they write their own prompt and see it work, the sale shifts from "show me" to "how do I get this?"
If they hesitate
"Give me the messiest column in any spreadsheet you have open. Let's clean it."
Lowers the barrier. Everyone has messy data. It's universally relatable.
After it works, ask
"How many people on your team would use this if it were installed on their machines tomorrow?"
This is a buying signal question disguised as curiosity. Their answer tells you deal size and urgency.
This is the most important phase. A customer who has typed their own prompt and seen it work on their own mental model of a problem will sell the product internally for you. They become the champion.
05
Close: anchor on the privacy story and next steps (3 min)
▶
Goal: handle the two biggest objections before they come up, then get a commitment
Objection 1: "Is our data safe?"
"Everything runs locally through LM Studio on your machine. No data leaves your network. No API calls to OpenAI. No cloud. Your IT team can audit the entire data flow."
This is your unfair advantage over ChatGPT-based competitors. Lead with it, don't wait for them to ask.
Objection 2: "How accurate is it?"
"You saw the results live. And because it's a formula, you can audit every output. It's right there in the cell, not hidden in some pipeline. Your team reviews it the same way they'd review any spreadsheet."
The transparency angle: it's not a black box. Every output is visible and auditable in the cell.
Closing question
"What would you need to see to run a pilot with your team next week?"
Doesn't ask "are you interested?" That's a yes/no gate. Instead asks "what's left?" which assumes the sale and surfaces the real blockers (IT approval, budget, etc.).
Expansion question
"Beyond your team, which other departments deal with large volumes of text data? Legal? Customer support? Marketing?"
Plants the seed for a larger deal. If they start listing departments, you've got internal expansion mapped.
Never end the demo on features. End on their words: "You said your team spends X hours on Y. We just did it in 30 seconds. What's the right next step?"
